## Post 10: Database Technology and Privacy

**Prompt:** Research the tension between database technology and privacy and discuss how this will or should play out as powerful database applications become more pervasive. Must include citations.

**My Response:**

**The Contradiction of Privacy**

A known happening in the digital world states that many individuals are concerned about their online privacy and the control they have over it, yet their online behavior suggests the opposite. This phenomenon is known as "The Privacy Paradox." In other words, many users prefer their digital data be kept private, but willingly use the internet in a way that gets their information stolen, sold, etc. for the sake of convenience or social interaction.

According to Zhu et al. (2025), the "privacy paradox" idea first stemmed from the medical field where researchers learned that patients did not want their privacy divulged, but a good explanation from someone in a respectable position could often sway their opinion. Studies also showed that the paradox mainly focused on a framework known as "Privacy Calculus" wherein users weigh the cost of their privacy vs. the benefit. To put it plainly, many of us will entirely skip the Terms and Conditions segment when we download a new app, buy a new device, create an account, or download an update for anything because we want to get to the good stuff. It is always there. The pages and pages of legal jargon that must be scrolled to the end before the little "I Agree" button will light up. We all know that button. I know I'm guilty of not reading that agreement the majority of the time. I, like many others, just want to skip past it so I can play a video game or use my phone again after an update. I could have just agreed to anything.

A communications professor at The University of Connecticut in 2016 actually ran an experiment amongst 543 communication students to sign up for a fake social media site. The experiment became rather comical when it came to agreeing to the Terms and Conditions during sign-up. Inside the agreement were a couple of clauses that gave the fake social media site two interesting rights over the user. The first was that the site may share any and all information with third parties that included the NSA and the second stated that the user would have to give their first-born child to the site. Apparently 98% of the 543 students agreed to both with 74% skipping all of the reading entirely in favor of the "quick join" button (Poitras, 2016).

In an article I found, the authors hypothesized that an "attitudinal ambivalence" (Zhu et al., 2025) influences choice when certain external forces arise. They also argue that companies will persuade users to give up their personal data in an elusive fashion. Think of a big company that makes a product you use every day. Chances are you agreed to their personal terms of service for the reward of using said service. This is what they prey on. If an individual actually sat down and read the thousands of words in that service agreement, they may find something being done with their data that they don't like, but they'll probably still agree because if they don't, they'll lose access to whatever it is the terms are guarding. The standards immediately go out the window because many need their phones or computers to function in daily life. Again, I know I've avoided reading these types of agreements because life simply would not work the same unless I do.

In fact, check out the "Acceptable Use" terms of Instructure. They are the company that owns and operates Canvas, the very platform we are using right now. Penn State maintains a working relationship with them where the terms are agreed to on our (the students) behalf. Regardless, the data we submit here (discussion posts, messages, etc.) are all governed by Instructure's policies. That is why our professors work so diligently to provide us with the information in our assignments and their announcements that we need to not just learn effectively, but to avoid breaking the rules as well.

Ultimately, while the technology we have does provide a world of benefit in many ways, one must always be aware of how their privacy might be manipulated. Every program and device we use has service terms that many of us agree to without reading and no second thought. Always ensure what you agree to is something that won't come back to haunt you later. Also, remember that the internet is forever. Nothing is expunged permanently and can be sold or used against you.

## Post 11: Crowdsourcing

**Prompt:** Choose two from seven prompts on crowdsourcing including its definition, benefits, challenges, types, real-world applications, ethical considerations, and future trends.

**My Response:**

**Prompt 2 - Benefits and Opportunities:**

Crowdsourcing's biggest strength is access.
